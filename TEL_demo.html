Design and Implementation of a Frisbee-Launching Mobile Robot

Competition Project Demo

Team Members: Luo Ping, Zhao Bairu, Hsu Tang-Wei Advisor: Prof. Tsai Hsi-Cheng Date: Jan. 8, 2025

Abstract

This project was inspired by the Tokyo Robotics Contest 2024, where the goal was to design a mobile robot capable of continuously launching frisbees into target baskets of varying heights (140 cm, 180 cm, and 220 cm). The robot was designed with omni-directional mobility, automatic frisbee feeding, and reliable frisbee shooting. Post-competition improvements focused on control systems and computer vision integration.

Mechanical Design

Dimensions: 70 × 70 × 80 cm | Weight: 45 kg | Frisbee Capacity: 20

  • Chassis: Aluminum extrusion frame with mecanum wheels for omni-directional movement. Motors with encoders provided closed-loop speed control.
  • Launching Track: A 45° angled track with rollers and rubber strips to provide spin and forward thrust. Driven by a CIM motor with servo-assisted feeding.
  • Storage & Feeding: Dual-barrel storage with a V-shaped feeder and spiral 3D-printed parts to ensure precise frisbee delivery into the launcher.

System Architecture

The robot integrates multiple subsystems coordinated by Jetson Nano, Arduino microcontrollers, and wireless control modules.

  • Vision System: Stereo camera setup for frisbee and basket detection, powered by YOLOv5 and stereo depth estimation.
  • Feeding & Launching: Servo and DC motors with encoders, controlled via L298N and BTS7960 drivers.
  • Mobility: Mecanum wheels driven by DC motors, with IMU-based orientation correction.
  • Wireless Control: AT9S remote and R9DS receiver using SBUS protocol, connected to Arduino for execution.
  • Signal Flow: Jetson Nano handles vision and trajectory prediction; Arduino executes motor commands and feedback control.

Communication System

SBUS protocol was selected over PWM for communication due to higher efficiency, multi-channel support, and minimal interference. An inverter circuit was added to handle SBUS signal inversion for Arduino processing.

Control System

  • Chassis Speed Control: Encoder feedback with error compensation ensured stable wheel synchronization.
  • Feeding Mechanism: Encoder-based DC motors allowed precise spiral rotation to release frisbees without jamming.
  • Orientation Correction: IMU (BNO055) calibration reduced drift to 0.1° over 20 minutes of operation.
  • Power Regulation: Voltage monitoring adjusted PWM outputs to maintain stable power supply for motors.

Vision System

The vision system employed stereo cameras and YOLOv5 detection to identify frisbees and baskets, followed by depth estimation and 3D trajectory fitting. An LSTM-based model was trained to predict frisbee flight paths using Z-axis (height) data, achieving prediction accuracy within ±10 cm under real test conditions.

Key Results

  • Reliable SBUS communication improved efficiency and reduced interference compared to PWM.
  • Wheel speed synchronization enhanced stability and precision in movement.
  • Vision-guided launching reduced trial-and-error and improved shooting accuracy.
  • LSTM trajectory prediction demonstrated potential for intelligent aiming in dynamic environments.

Conclusion

This project successfully integrated mechanical design, control systems, and vision-based trajectory prediction to build a cost-effective frisbee-launching robot. Through continuous experimentation and optimization, the team achieved competition goals and gained hands-on experience in robotics integration, vision systems, and control design.
